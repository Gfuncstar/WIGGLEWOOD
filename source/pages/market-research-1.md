## Body copy
- Wiggle Wood’s developmental approach is informed in consultation with child development specialist Dr. Jacqueline Harding , author of The Brain that Loves to Play (2024) and a recognised authority in early childhood neuroscience, play-based learning, and school readiness. Dr. Harding has contributed to national conversations on early years development and has worked alongside education and policy bodies supporting frameworks aligned with the UK’s Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) , which guides best practice in communication development, emotional wellbeing, and early learning environments.
- Through her work with Middlesex University’s Tomorrow’s Child research initiative , Harding has explored how children engage with digital media environments and how parents navigate screen-based learning. Her research highlights the importance of intentional, developmentally appropriate digital content that supports rather than overwhelms young audiences, reinforcing the need for carefully designed storytelling that nurtures confidence, emotional security, and curiosity in early childhood.
- Her wider research demonstrates that young children’s brains are biologically wired to learn through play, imagination, music, and emotionally engaging experiences, with repeated positive interactions strengthening neural pathways linked to communication, self-regulation, and social understanding. Harding’s work also emphasises that children begin forming beliefs about identity, capability, and self-worth from an early age, reinforcing the importance of storytelling environments that model resilience, kindness, and self-acceptance.
- These insights are supported by wider academic research demonstrating that early childhood is a period of rapid brain development, during which repeated experiences significantly influence language acquisition and emotional development ( Center on the Developing Child, Harvard University, 2016 ). Studies into preschool viewing behaviours show that repetition supports comprehension and confidence, with landmark research into early childhood programming such as Blue’s Clues demonstrating that repeated exposure increased engagement, participation, and problem-solving confidence among young viewers ( Anderson et al., University of Massachusetts, 2000 ).
- Music also plays a significant role in early childhood development. Research published in Psychology of Music found that musical engagement supports emotional connection, memory formation, and language development, with rhythm and repetition helping young children anticipate patterns and participate actively ( Gerry, Unrau & Trainor, McMaster University, 2012 ).
- Preschool audiences are consistently shown to respond positively to:
- bright, high-contrast visual environments that support recognition and sustained attention ( Franklin et al., Sussex Baby Lab, 2010 )
- expressive characters with clearly readable emotions that support empathy development ( Denham, Emotional Development in Early Childhood, 1998 )
- simple narrative structures that resolve emotional tension in reassuring ways ( Applebee, The Child’s Concept of Story, 1978 )
- repetition that strengthens familiarity, confidence, and enjoyment ( Anderson & Pempek, American Behavioral Scientist, 2005 )
- Research from Common Sense Media (Rideout & Robb, 2020) confirms that children aged 2–5 frequently choose to watch familiar content repeatedly, finding comfort in predictable story patterns and character relationships. Despite their age, preschool viewers are highly discerning: content that does not quickly capture emotional or sensory engagement is often rejected, whereas content that resonates can lead to strong repeat viewing behaviour and long-term brand affinity.
- While children drive repeat viewing behaviour, parents and caregivers act as the primary gatekeepers of content selection. In an increasingly complex digital environment, parents are balancing the practical role of screen-based entertainment with growing concern about content quality, emotional impact, and online safety.
- Research from Ofcom’s Children and Parents: Media Use and Attitudes Report (2023) shows that parents prioritise trusted, age-appropriate programming that models positive behaviour and supports emotional wellbeing. Similarly, Common Sense Media’s The Common Sense Census (2020) indicates that screen media is now embedded within everyday family life, reflecting the realities of busy households managing competing demands on time.
- Academic research published in the Journal of Children and Media (Nikken & Schols, 2015) highlights that parents actively seek content aligned with values such as kindness, empathy, inclusion, and confidence-building. Co-viewing is also recognised as beneficial, with research from the Joan Ganz Cooney Center (Takeuchi & Stevens, 2011) demonstrating that shared media experiences can support language development, emotional learning, and parent-child connection.
- As a result, demand continues to grow for programming that parents perceive as safe, thoughtful, and emotionally supportive, while still being entertaining enough to sustain children’s attention independently.
- Guided by recognised child development expertise and supported by established academic research, Wiggle Wood is intentionally designed to align with the developmental preferences of preschool audiences while meeting the expectations of modern caregivers. Its combination of music-led storytelling, expressive character design, gentle humour, and repeatable narrative structure reflects well-documented engagement patterns observed across successful preschool programming.
- By balancing joyful entertainment with emotional reassurance, Wiggle Wood supports both child enjoyment and parent confidence. This dual-audience alignment strengthens the brand’s positioning within an increasingly quality-conscious children’s media landscape, where trust, repeat engagement, and positive values are key drivers of long-term audience loyalty.
