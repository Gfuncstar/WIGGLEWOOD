Market Research Overview

Wiggle Wood’s market research demonstrates that the pathway to building a global children’s IP through free-to-access digital distribution is already proven. Breakout successes such as CoComelon and Blippi show that even independently created content can scale rapidly on YouTube, achieving significant enterprise value without substantial upfront investment. Wiggle Wood applies this proven route to market with clear strategic intent, combining premium-quality production, dedicated marketing investment, and an experienced team from the outset. Together, the SWOT analysis, competitor landscape, and strategic positioning below evidence a rare alignment of timing, demand, and model innovation, positioning Wiggle Wood to scale faster and more deliberately than previous digital-first children’s brands.

Competitor Landscape Summary

Wiggle Wood enters a competitive but opportunity-rich preschool landscape, where established giants like PAW Patrol, Bluey, Peppa Pig, CoComelon, Hey Duggee! and Blippi dominate across traditional and digital platforms. However, each of these very successful incumbents reveals gaps and insights that Wiggle Wood is uniquely positioned to fill and maximise.

PAW Patrol is the titan of children’s IP worth over $15bn. Launched in 2013 by Spin Master, PAW Patrol pioneered an integrated IP model in which storytelling and merchandise were developed in tandem from the outset, allowing toy design, character creation, and narrative to inform one another. This synchronised approach enabled rapid global retail rollout and sustained product innovation aligned to evolving story themes. The result is one of the most commercially successful preschool franchises in history, generating over $15 billion in consumer product sales worldwide. However, the criticism of the show is that the storytelling is secondary to the show’s heavy focus on integrating products into each episode. Meanwhile, Wiggle Wood mirrors the “merchandise-centric” approach, but leads with rich storytelling and powerful musicality.

Bluey (the fastest-growing children’s IP, launched in 2019 and now worth $4.4bn) has redefined emotional authenticity in preschool content, with deep parent-child storytelling, a focus on humour targeting parents, and global Disney+ distribution. Yet it operates within a fixed-format, broadcast-to-streaming model and has no musical element other than its score. The show has been criticised for being designed as much for parents as for children. Wiggle Wood, by contrast, is made for children, helping them navigate their world on their own terms. It builds on the emotional resonance of Bluey but adds musical engagement, nature-based themes, and YouTube-first adaptability, creating a more iterative and accessible brand.

Peppa Pig (worth $1.7bn) remains a global merchandising powerhouse, driven by simple, repetitive storytelling and iconic character design. Yet, its format is rooted in linear television and lacks the emotional depth and musical innovation today’s parents increasingly seek. In contrast, Wiggle Wood offers a more values-forward narrative with rich emotional storytelling and integrated music, better aligned with modern family priorities.

CoComelon was launched in 2006 on YouTube, securing huge success in 2015, rebranded in 2018 and acquired for $3bn in 2021. It is the undisputed leader in music-driven, algorithmic YouTube content. However, despite its huge success of 4bn monthly views on YouTube, its short-form, repetition-based model lacks narrative and character depth. On the other hand, Wiggle Wood differentiates itself by combining the stickiness of original music with strong character arcs and story-led structure, delivering both entertainment and emotional value.

Hey Duggee! (launched in 2014) showed clear potential to become a major global children’s IP, supported by its distinctive visual style, gentle educational themes, and layered humour that earned a loyal following among both children and parents. However, as a BBC-originated property, it did not benefit from sustained marketing investment or the high-frequency repeat scheduling often used to build rapid audience familiarity in preschool content. This limited the speed at which the brand could scale internationally and expand into a broader consumer products ecosystem. Wiggle Wood is not constrained by traditional broadcaster limitations on marketing exposure or scheduling prioritisation. As a self-distributed brand, Wiggle Wood retains full control over audience data, campaign strategy, and promotional timing, allowing sustained investment behind all episodes rather than only initial launches.

Blippi (created on YouTube in 2014, acquired by Moonbug in 2020 for an estimated $120 million) demonstrates how a digitally native children’s brand can scale rapidly through YouTube-first distribution before expanding into merchandise, streaming platforms, and live experiences. At peak growth, Blippi was estimated to generate approximately $9–17 million annually from YouTube revenue alone. Developed independently by a single creator without an initial strategy to build a global franchise, it illustrates the scale that can emerge organically. By contrast, Wiggle Wood is being built with clear strategic intent, backed by an experienced, high-performance creative team, structured investment, and an integrated brand approach designed from the outset to maximise long-term global value.

Strategic Positioning within the Market

Wiggle Wood is positioned as a category-bridging innovator, blending Bluey’s emotional depth and humour with CoComelon’s musical appeal and PAW Patrol’s merchandise focus. It is the first purpose-built, high-production value YouTube-native preschool IP and the first to integrate original music as a narrative driver rather than an add-on. While digital-first breakout successes such as CoComelon and Blippi demonstrate the scale that can be achieved through YouTube-native distribution, Wiggle Wood is the first to apply this proven pathway with deliberate creative, commercial, and investment strategy from the outset.

This unique model is reinforced by a live data and testing feedback loop via YouTube as the primary platform, enabling real-time audience insight and continuous creative refinement, something none of its competitors currently offer. Crucially, Wiggle Wood is more closely aligned with the values of today’s parents: kindness, emotional wellbeing, environmental awareness, and mindfulness. With a built-in plan for fast, scalable global reach, cross-platform monetisation, and community-building through music and character-led content, Wiggle Wood is not just entering the preschool space, it is reshaping what success in this category can look like.

Wiggle Wood benefits from a series of structural strengths that position it distinctively within the children’s content market. Strategic early partnerships include Lasso Group as YouTube distribution partner, bringing the cross-promotional advantage of Little Amigo, a preschool channel with over 700,000 subscribers and over 3 million views. Audience insight is embedded throughout development via market research specialists DiO and child development expert Dr Jacqueline Harding, ensuring creative and commercial decisions are consistently evidence-led. The project is supported by established legal and IP frameworks, an experienced global licensing sales agent, and a high-calibre creative team featuring BAFTA, Grammy and Emmy-nominated and winning talent. Wiggle Wood also benefits from an extended network of culturally relevant influencers, including Musical Director Jax Jones and Chadwick’s Voice Actress Corinna Brown, with a combined social reach exceeding 22.5 million followers.

SWOT Analysis

Strengths: High-quality animation at scale, world-class creative team, YouTube-first format enabling agile storytelling, platform optimisation, and real-time responsiveness to audience behaviour. Scalable plans across multiple languages and a diversified revenue model spanning animation, music, publishing, merchandise, and live experiences. The IP is designed for efficient global expansion and strong long-term commercial potential.

Weaknesses: High-quality animation is a premium medium, requiring meaningful upfront investment and disciplined production management. The model also requires a significant initial content pipeline to support consistent weekly publishing rhythms aligned with YouTube’s algorithm, meaning development timelines must be carefully managed to maintain momentum toward launch. Building global brand awareness from inception requires coordinated effort across creative, marketing, and commercial teams, alongside sustained investment during the early growth phase.

Opportunities: The industry is actively seeking new original IP, as broadcasters reduce commissioning spend and shift toward acquisition-led strategies, leaving a clear gap for independently financed, globally scalable children’s brands. The UK’s 39% animation tax credit creates a particularly favourable environment for production. Early investment also accelerates trademark expansion and brand protection internationally, reducing the risk of competitive imitation as the property gains visibility. With strong demand for meaningful, values-led preschool content and proven digital-first pathways to scale, Wiggle Wood is well positioned to move quickly and establish category presence.

Threats: External threats include an increasingly competitive YouTube landscape, evolving platform dynamics, and wider economic pressures affecting investor confidence across the media sector. These risks are mitigated through diversified revenue streams, bonded production oversight, disciplined cost management, and a strategy designed to build long-term enterprise value beyond any single platform dependency. Supported by an experienced leadership team and a clearly defined commercial roadmap, Wiggle Wood is positioned to navigate complexity while capitalising on a rare alignment of opportunity, timing, and strategic intent.

Audience Insights

Primary Audience: Children aged 3–5

Wiggle Wood’s developmental approach is informed in consultation with child development specialist Dr Jacqueline Harding, author of The Brain that Loves to Play (2024) and a recognised authority in early childhood neuroscience, play-based learning, and school readiness. Her wider research demonstrates that young children’s brains are biologically wired to learn through play, imagination, music, and emotionally engaging experiences, with repeated positive interactions strengthening neural pathways linked to communication, self-regulation, and social understanding. Harding’s work also emphasises that children begin forming beliefs about identity, capability, and self-worth from an early age, reinforcing the importance of storytelling environments that model resilience, kindness, and self-acceptance.

Preschool audiences are consistently shown to respond positively to: bright, high-contrast visual environments that support recognition and sustained attention; expressive characters with clearly readable emotions that support empathy development; simple narrative structures that resolve emotional tension in reassuring ways; and repetition that strengthens familiarity, confidence, and enjoyment. Research from Common Sense Media confirms that children aged 2–5 frequently choose to watch familiar content repeatedly, finding comfort in predictable story patterns and character relationships.

Music also plays a significant role in early childhood development. Research published in Psychology of Music found that musical engagement supports emotional connection, memory formation, and language development, with rhythm and repetition helping young children anticipate patterns and participate actively.

Secondary Audience: Parents and Caregivers

While children drive repeat viewing behaviour, parents and caregivers act as the primary gatekeepers of content selection. Research from Ofcom’s Children and Parents: Media Use and Attitudes Report shows that parents prioritise trusted, age-appropriate programming that models positive behaviour and supports emotional wellbeing. Academic research highlights that parents actively seek content aligned with values such as kindness, empathy, inclusion, and confidence-building. Co-viewing is also recognised as beneficial, with research demonstrating that shared media experiences can support language development, emotional learning, and parent-child connection.

Implications for Wiggle Wood

Guided by recognised child development expertise and supported by established academic research, Wiggle Wood is intentionally designed to align with the developmental preferences of preschool audiences while meeting the expectations of modern caregivers. Its combination of music-led storytelling, expressive character design, gentle humour, and repeatable narrative structure reflects well-documented engagement patterns observed across successful preschool programming. By balancing joyful entertainment with emotional reassurance, Wiggle Wood supports both child enjoyment and parent confidence, strengthening the brand’s positioning within an increasingly quality-conscious children’s media landscape where trust, repeat engagement, and positive values are key drivers of long-term audience loyalty.

In preparation for full production, Wiggle Wood commissioned independent pilot testing with 100 parents of children aged 2 to 7 across the UK. Participants watched the completed 8-minute pilot episode in full before completing the research. The results are unambiguous. The research was conducted by DiO, an independent research organisation with a long-standing relationship with Hartree Centre in Liverpool, one of Europe’s largest multidisciplinary scientific research organisations. DiO deployed a combination of quantitative survey data, qualitative feedback, and facial coding technology to measure emotional response in real time.

Audience Approval

89% of parents said they would be happy for their child to watch Wiggle Wood. 77% of the target audience said they would watch more episodes right now. And 71% said they wanted it made into a full series. These are not measures of mild appreciation. They are expressions of genuine demand from the exact audience Wiggle Wood is built for.

“This will be a CBeebies hit.” — Direct quote from a parent who watched the pilot

Benchmarking Against the Best

69% of parents rated Wiggle Wood as good as or better than their child’s current favourite show, with Bluey named as the top comparison in the study. Among parents whose child already watches Bluey, 92% said they would be happy for their child to watch Wiggle Wood. To benchmark this strongly against one of the most successful and beloved preschool franchises in the world, based on a single pilot episode, is a remarkable validation of both the creative quality and the commercial potential of the IP.

Standing Out in a Crowded Market

60% of parents said Wiggle Wood fills a gap in the market, and 59% said it stands out from other preschool shows. In a landscape where established giants dominate viewing habits and new entrants rarely cut through, this finding speaks directly to the strength of Wiggle Wood’s positioning as an emotionally intelligent, music-led, nature-based series with a genuinely distinctive identity.

Music as a Commercial Driver

68% of parents said their child would move or dance to the Wiggle Wood music, rising to 73% among parents of under-5s. 58.8% said their child would sing along. Songs and music were cited by 55% of parents as one of the things they loved most about the show, making it the second highest positive theme in the entire study, behind only educational and life lesson content.

Merchandise Potential

50% of all parents tested said they would buy Wiggle Wood merchandise for their child. DiO confirmed that merchandise is typically a reluctant purchase, and most shows do not chart at this level in concept testing. This result is, in their assessment, a very positive commercial signal and a meaningful early indicator of the consumer products opportunity available to Wiggle Wood as the brand scales.

The Wind-Down Moment

84.7% of parents rated the wind-down segment at the end of the episode 4 or 5 out of 5. Parents independently and unprompted placed Wiggle Wood into the 6pm CBeebies wind-down slot, confirming the show is meeting a real need in the household routine. Facial coding data recorded an engagement peak at the very close of the wind-down sequence. This validates one of Wiggle Wood’s most distinctive creative choices: the decision to close each episode with a gentle, mindfulness-led transition rather than a high-energy finish.

Facial Coding: No Low Moments

DiO used facial coding technology to measure parents’ emotional responses throughout the episode in real time. Engagement rose consistently across the full 8 minutes. The highest emotional peaks clustered around the musical numbers and the closing wind-down sequence, with additional standout moments around Luna’s glow returning and Chadwick’s hero scene. No low moments were recorded at any point. The episode was also found to have a genuinely calming effect on viewers. Facial coding is a sophisticated research methodology rarely used in independent children’s content testing at this stage of development.

What This Means

This research was conducted on a single completed pilot episode, before full production investment. The results benchmark Wiggle Wood against the biggest preschool brands in the world, confirm strong merchandise potential, validate the music strategy, and demonstrate that the emotional design of the show is working exactly as intended. In May 2026, Dr Jacqueline Harding conducted a formal mixed-method study evaluating the developmental, emotional, and educational impact of the pilot episode, Show Your Glow, on children aged 3 to 8. The research was conducted across two schools and a home setting, with over 130 children participating, alongside 10 educators, 5 parents, and 3 grandparents.

About Dr Jacqueline Harding

Dr Jacqueline Harding MA, Cert Ed, Cert Clinical Neurophysiology, SFHEA is an international child development and education expert, recognised for her work advising the UK government, practitioners, and parents. She is an Honorary Visiting Research Fellow at Middlesex University and Chair of the Bright Start Foundation. She has worked on some of the most celebrated children’s television productions in the world, including Peter Rabbit, Teletubbies, Clangers, Bing, and BeddyByes.

Dr Harding used the Tomorrow’s Child Assessment Tool — her proprietary methodology, applied internationally across more than 30 television productions. Notably, she is selective about the productions she agrees to assess. In her own words: “My heart is: is this going to help children or not? If it’s just screen fodder, I’m not interested.”

The Headline Result

The Tomorrow’s Child Assessment Tool measures attention and engagement at five stages across an episode, each scored out of 12. The maximum total score is 60. A successful, high-performing children’s television show typically scores between 49 and 50. Wiggle Wood scored 12/12 at every stage. A total of 60/60. According to Dr Harding, this is the first time a perfect score has been awarded across her 20-plus years of research.

Comprehension and Narrative Understanding

Children demonstrated unusually high comprehension on a single, first viewing, with no prior exposure to the characters or world. Ages 4–5: 80% comprehension. Ages 5–6: 75% comprehension. Ages 6–7: 80% comprehension. Children were able to recall narrative structure, describe emotional themes, explain character motivations, and articulate the concept of “wiggling it out” as emotional self-regulation.

What the Research Tells Us About the Show

From the documentary film produced alongside the formal report, Dr Harding observes: “Right from the very start, one thing became clear: this programme truly understands children. Not simply as viewers, not simply as consumers, but as young human beings whose brains, emotions and sense of self are still under construction. And that matters enormously.”

On “wiggling it out”: “Children regulate through movement long before they regulate through reasoning. Their bodies help organise their brains. So when a child is invited to wiggle it out, they’re being given a practical, emotionally safe strategy their nervous system can genuinely use. That’s where the gold dust lies.”

Humour and Music

Humour sequences consistently generated spontaneous, contagious laughter across all age groups and settings, with children mimicking moments and turning to each other to share the reaction. Music emerged as a major strength. Children responded physically throughout, moving, wiggling, singing along. The closing song, Your Best Is Beautiful, produced visible emotional responses in both children and the adult educators present.

The Wind-Down and Emotional Regulation

The mindful wind-down sequence was identified as one of the programme’s most important differentiators. Researchers observed reduced post-viewing dysregulation compared to typical screen content, calm transitions following the episode, an absence of screen fatigue or overstimulation, and increased emotional stillness and reflective behaviour in children.

One educator observed: “Just to have those little tricks up their sleeve to help them transform from one phase and feeling to another is a good strength for them to have. I love the fact that at the end of the programme it asks the children to close their eyes, to think of something, to listen. All emotions are real and valid, and being able to name them is important. But just as importantly, children need ways to deal with their emotions.”

Emotional Depth

The programme’s core emotional message — that it is safe to feel your feelings, and that you can move through them — was widely and spontaneously understood. One child experiencing emotional distress at the time of viewing described what the programme gave them as: “Taking the bad stuff out of my head.”

SEND Representation

The research included meaningful representation of children with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities. In one placement, SEND representation reached 49% of the children present. The programme performed strongly across this group, with no meaningful difference in engagement or response compared to the wider cohort.

Character Connection

Children formed strong attachments to the characters on a single viewing. Bellona emerged as the most consistently preferred character across all age groups, followed closely by Chadwick. Younger children recalled character names readily; older children engaged more with personality, motivation, and narrative role — all indicators of genuine connection rather than surface recognition.

Dr Harding’s Conclusion

“The evidence strongly suggests that Wiggle Wood has significant developmental and educational potential and represents a valuable contribution to children’s broadcasting. Wiggle Wood occupies a unique and valuable place within contemporary children’s media. Children deserve media experiences that respect their intelligence, emotional depth, and developmental capacity.” — Dr Jacqueline Harding, May 2026

Independent research of this quality is rare at any stage of production, let alone at pilot. A perfect engagement score, comprehension rates of 75–80% on first viewing, and immediate emotional connection across children from diverse socioeconomic and SEN backgrounds is direct proof of concept.
