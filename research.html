Industry Trends & Opportunities

The children’s content landscape is undergoing a profound shift. YouTube has won the children’s audience, and the industry has accepted it. Not as a passing disruption, but as a permanent structural shift. Every major broadcaster is now responding to a world in which young children watch YouTube first, and television is no longer their default.

In a move that sent shockwaves through the UK’s creative industry, in July 2025 Sky announced it will no longer commission original programming for children, opting instead to licence cheaper, pre-made series. With Channel 5’s Milkshake operating on limited budgets and the BBC now standing as the only major commissioner of UK children’s content, the market for original, values-led programming is rapidly narrowing.

At the same time, demand from families has never been higher. Over 4 million children in the UK are living in poverty, and for many households, YouTube is the most accessible platform for children’s entertainment. But while the content is free and abundant, much of that content is unregulated, addictive, and lacking the care, creativity and emotional nourishment that young minds deserve.

This creates a rare window of opportunity for independent creators like Wiggle Wood.

Wiggle Wood steps into the space left behind by traditional broadcasters, offering something original, joyful, and deeply intentional: a nature-based animated series that entertains, uplifts, and supports healthy early development. With each 10-minute episode delivering music, life lessons, and genuine emotional warmth, the series is built for both the developmental needs of young children and the content expectations of modern parents.

For Wiggle Wood, this isn’t just a mission - it’s also smart business.

Historically, a new global preschool IP tends to emerge every four to six years, reflecting the constant renewal of young audiences and the need for fresh storytelling. Major breakout brands include Peppa Pig (2004), PAW Patrol (2013), CoComelon (2015), and Bluey (2019), each achieving significant global scale through distinctive storytelling and commercial strategies. Industry experts widely recognise that the market is now due the next major original preschool brand, particularly as reliance on legacy franchises continues to increase.

Wiggle Wood is designed specifically for this window of opportunity, combining digital-first distribution, repeat-viewing accessibility, and sustained marketing investment to accelerate audience growth and long-term brand value. When buyers, platforms, and broadcasters seek to rebuild their offering with meaningful, British-made content, Wiggle Wood will be ready: established, audience-proven, and acquisition-ready.

Market Research Overview

Wiggle Wood’s market research demonstrates that the pathway to building a global children’s IP through free-to-access digital distribution is already proven. Breakout successes such as CoComelon and Blippi show that even independently created content can scale rapidly on YouTube, achieving significant enterprise value without substantial upfront investment. Wiggle Wood applies this proven route to market with clear strategic intent, combining premium-quality production, dedicated marketing investment, and an experienced team from the outset.

Competitor Landscape Summary

PAW Patrol (worth over $15bn) pioneered an integrated IP model in which storytelling and merchandise were developed in tandem from the outset, allowing toy design, character creation, and narrative to inform one another. This synchronised approach enabled rapid global retail rollout and sustained product innovation aligned to evolving story themes. The result is one of the most commercially successful preschool franchises in history. However, the criticism of the show is that the storytelling is secondary to the show’s heavy focus on integrating products into each episode. Wiggle Wood mirrors the “merchandise-centric” approach, but leads with rich storytelling and powerful musicality.

Bluey (now worth $4.4bn) has redefined emotional authenticity in preschool content, with deep parent-child storytelling and global Disney+ distribution. Yet it operates within a fixed-format, broadcast-to-streaming model and has no musical element other than its score. The show has been criticised for being designed as much for parents as for children. Wiggle Wood, by contrast, is made for children, helping them navigate their world on their own terms. It builds on the emotional resonance of Bluey but adds musical engagement, nature-based themes, and YouTube-first adaptability.

Peppa Pig (worth $1.7bn) remains a global merchandising powerhouse, driven by simple, repetitive storytelling and iconic character design. Yet its format is rooted in linear television and lacks the emotional depth and musical innovation today’s parents increasingly seek. Wiggle Wood offers a more values-forward narrative with rich emotional storytelling and integrated music, better aligned with modern family priorities.

CoComelon (acquired for $3bn in 2021) is the undisputed leader in music-driven, algorithmic YouTube content, with 4 billion monthly views. However, its short-form, repetition-based model lacks narrative and character depth. Wiggle Wood differentiates itself by combining the stickiness of original music with strong character arcs and story-led structure, delivering both entertainment and emotional value.

Hey Duggee! (2014) showed clear potential to become a major global children’s IP, but as a BBC-originated property did not benefit from sustained marketing investment or high-frequency repeat scheduling. This limited the speed at which the brand could scale internationally. Wiggle Wood is not constrained by traditional broadcaster limitations on marketing exposure or scheduling prioritisation. As a self-distributed brand, Wiggle Wood retains full control over audience data, campaign strategy, and promotional timing.

Blippi (acquired by Moonbug in 2020 for an estimated $120 million) demonstrates how a digitally native children’s brand can scale rapidly through YouTube-first distribution. At peak growth, Blippi was estimated to generate approximately $9–17 million annually from YouTube revenue alone. Developed independently by a single creator without an initial strategy to build a global franchise, Blippi illustrates the scale that can emerge organically. By contrast, Wiggle Wood is being built with clear strategic intent, backed by an experienced, high-performance creative team and structured investment.

Strategic Positioning within the Market

Wiggle Wood is positioned as a category-bridging innovator, blending Bluey’s emotional depth and humour with CoComelon’s musical appeal and PAW Patrol’s merchandise focus. It is the first purpose-built, high-production value YouTube-native preschool IP and the first to integrate original music as a narrative driver rather than an add-on.

This unique model is reinforced by a live data and testing feedback loop via YouTube as the primary platform, enabling real-time audience insight and continuous creative refinement - something none of its competitors currently offer. Crucially, Wiggle Wood is more closely aligned with the values of today’s parents: kindness, emotional wellbeing, environmental awareness, and mindfulness.

SWOT Analysis

Strengths: High-quality animation at scale, world-class creative team, YouTube-first format enabling agile storytelling and real-time audience responsiveness, diversified revenue model, fully owned IP, embedded audience insight via DiO and Dr Jacqueline Harding.

Weaknesses: High-quality animation requires meaningful upfront investment; significant initial content pipeline required to support weekly publishing rhythms; building global brand awareness requires coordinated effort and sustained investment during the early growth phase.

Opportunities: Industry actively seeking new original IP as broadcasters reduce commissioning spend; UK’s 39% animation tax credit creates a favourable production environment; strong demand for values-led preschool content; proven digital-first pathways to scale.

Threats: Increasingly competitive YouTube landscape; evolving platform dynamics; wider economic pressures affecting investor confidence. These risks are mitigated through diversified revenue streams, bonded production oversight, disciplined cost management, and a strategy designed to build long-term enterprise value beyond any single platform dependency.

Audience Insights

Primary Audience: Children aged 3–5

Wiggle Wood’s developmental approach is informed in consultation with child development specialist Dr Jacqueline Harding, author of The Brain that Loves to Play (2024) and a recognised authority in early childhood neuroscience, play-based learning, and school readiness. Through her work with Middlesex University’s Tomorrow’s Child research initiative, Harding has explored how children engage with digital media environments and how parents navigate screen-based learning. Her research highlights the importance of intentional, developmentally appropriate digital content that supports rather than overwhelms young audiences.

Her wider research demonstrates that young children’s brains are biologically wired to learn through play, imagination, music, and emotionally engaging experiences, with repeated positive interactions strengthening neural pathways linked to communication, self-regulation, and social understanding. Harding’s work also emphasises that children begin forming beliefs about identity, capability, and self-worth from an early age, reinforcing the importance of storytelling environments that model resilience, kindness, and self-acceptance.

These insights are supported by wider academic research demonstrating that early childhood is a period of rapid brain development, during which repeated experiences significantly influence language acquisition and emotional development (Center on the Developing Child, Harvard University, 2016). Studies into preschool viewing behaviours show that repetition supports comprehension and confidence, with landmark research into early childhood programming such as Blue’s Clues demonstrating that repeated exposure increased engagement, participation, and problem-solving confidence among young viewers.

Music also plays a significant role in early childhood development. Research published in Psychology of Music found that musical engagement supports emotional connection, memory formation, and language development, with rhythm and repetition helping young children anticipate patterns and participate actively.

Preschool audiences are consistently shown to respond positively to bright, high-contrast visual environments that support recognition and sustained attention; expressive characters with clearly readable emotions that support empathy development; simple narrative structures that resolve emotional tension in reassuring ways; and repetition that strengthens familiarity, confidence, and enjoyment. Research from Common Sense Media confirms that children aged 2–5 frequently choose to watch familiar content repeatedly, finding comfort in predictable story patterns and character relationships.

Secondary Audience: Parents and Caregivers

While children drive repeat viewing behaviour, parents and caregivers act as the primary gatekeepers of content selection. In an increasingly complex digital environment, parents are balancing the practical role of screen-based entertainment with growing concern about content quality, emotional impact, and online safety.

Research from Ofcom’s Children and Parents: Media Use and Attitudes Report (2023) shows that parents prioritise trusted, age-appropriate programming that models positive behaviour and supports emotional wellbeing. Common Sense Media’s The Common Sense Census (2020) indicates that screen media is now embedded within everyday family life, reflecting the realities of busy households managing competing demands on time.

Academic research published in the Journal of Children and Media highlights that parents actively seek content aligned with values such as kindness, empathy, inclusion, and confidence-building. Co-viewing is also recognised as beneficial, with research from the Joan Ganz Cooney Center demonstrating that shared media experiences can support language development, emotional learning, and parent-child connection.

As a result, demand continues to grow for programming that parents perceive as safe, thoughtful, and emotionally supportive, while still being entertaining enough to sustain children’s attention independently.

Implications for Wiggle Wood

Guided by recognised child development expertise and supported by established academic research, Wiggle Wood is intentionally designed to align with the developmental preferences of preschool audiences while meeting the expectations of modern caregivers. Its combination of music-led storytelling, expressive character design, gentle humour, and repeatable narrative structure reflects well-documented engagement patterns observed across successful preschool programming. By balancing joyful entertainment with emotional reassurance, Wiggle Wood supports both child enjoyment and parent confidence, strengthening the brand’s positioning within an increasingly quality-conscious children’s media landscape where trust, repeat engagement, and positive values are key drivers of long-term audience loyalty.

Participants watched the completed 8-minute pilot episode in full before completing the research. The results are unambiguous. Audience Approval

89% of parents said they would be happy for their child to watch Wiggle Wood. 77% of the target audience said they would watch more episodes right now. And 71% said they wanted it made into a full series. These are not measures of mild appreciation. They are expressions of genuine demand from the exact audience Wiggle Wood is built for.

Benchmarking Against the Best

69% of parents rated Wiggle Wood as good as or better than their child’s current favourite show, with Bluey named as the top comparison in the study. Among parents whose child already watches Bluey, 92% said they would be happy for their child to watch Wiggle Wood. To benchmark this strongly against one of the most successful and beloved preschool franchises in the world, based on a single pilot episode and before full production investment has been made, is a remarkable validation of both the creative quality and the commercial potential of the IP.

Standing Out in a Crowded Market

60% of parents said Wiggle Wood fills a gap in the market, and 59% said it stands out from other preschool shows. In a landscape where established giants dominate viewing habits and new entrants rarely cut through, this finding speaks directly to the strength of Wiggle Wood’s positioning as an emotionally intelligent, music-led, nature-based series with a genuinely distinctive identity.

Music as a Commercial Driver

Music has always been central to the Wiggle Wood strategy, and the pilot testing confirms it is landing exactly as intended. 68% of parents said their child would move or dance to the Wiggle Wood music, rising to 73% among parents of under-5s. 58.8% said their child would sing along. These are strong indicators of the kind of active, participatory engagement that drives repeat viewing, playlist behaviour, and music catalogue value over time. Songs and music were cited by 55% of parents as one of the things they loved most about the show, making it the second highest positive theme in the entire study, behind only educational and life lesson content.

Merchandise Potential

50% of all parents tested said they would buy Wiggle Wood merchandise for their child. DiO confirmed that merchandise is typically a reluctant purchase, and most shows do not chart at this level in concept testing. This result is, in their assessment, a very positive commercial signal and a meaningful early indicator of the consumer products opportunity available to Wiggle Wood as the brand scales.

The Wind-Down Moment

84.7% of parents rated the wind-down segment at the end of the episode 4 or 5 out of 5. Parents independently and unprompted placed Wiggle Wood into the 6pm CBeebies wind-down slot, confirming the show is meeting a real need in the household routine. Facial coding data recorded an engagement peak at the very close of the wind-down sequence. This validates one of Wiggle Wood’s most distinctive creative choices: the decision to close each episode with a gentle, mindfulness-led transition rather than a high-energy finish. Content that actively supports the shift toward calm, rather than working against it.

Facial Coding: No Low Moments

DiO used facial coding technology to measure parents’ emotional responses throughout the episode in real time. Engagement rose consistently across the full 8 minutes. The highest emotional peaks clustered around the musical numbers and the closing wind-down sequence, with additional standout moments around Luna’s glow returning and Chadwick’s hero scene. No low moments were recorded at any point. The episode was also found to have a genuinely calming effect on viewers. Facial coding is a sophisticated research methodology rarely used in independent children’s content testing at this stage of development. Its findings here are unambiguous: Wiggle Wood does not just entertain. It actively supports the emotional wellbeing of everyone in the room.

What This Means

This research was conducted on a single completed pilot episode, before full production investment. The results benchmark Wiggle Wood against the biggest preschool brands in the world, confirm strong merchandise potential, validate the music strategy, and demonstrate that the emotional design of the show is working exactly as intended. Pilot Testing Results – Children

Conducted by Dr Jacqueline Harding

Wiggle Wood has been independently tested with 130 children by one of the UK’s most respected child development researchers. The findings are remarkable, and they speak for themselves.

In May 2026, Dr Jacqueline Harding conducted a formal mixed-method study evaluating the developmental, emotional, and educational impact of our pilot episode, Show Your Glow, on children aged 3 to 8 years. The research was conducted across two schools and a home setting, with over 130 children participating, alongside 10 educators, 5 parents, and 3 grandparents.

About Dr Jacqueline Harding

Dr Jacqueline Harding MA, Cert Ed, Cert Clinical Neurophysiology, SFHEA, is an international child development and education expert, recognised for her work advising the UK government, practitioners, and parents. She is an Honorary Visiting Research Fellow at Middlesex University and Chair of the Bright Start Foundation.

Jacqueline has worked on some of the most celebrated children’s television productions in the world, including Peter Rabbit, Teletubbies, Clangers, Bing, and BeddyByes, and is a contributor to CBeebies Parenting. She is a former BBC Education Editor, Government Consultant, and Headteacher.

Dr Harding’s research used the Tomorrow’s Child Assessment Tool - her proprietary methodology, applied internationally across more than 30 television productions to assess developmental suitability and engagement.

Notably, Dr Harding is selective about the productions she agrees to assess. In her own words: “My heart is: is this going to help children or not? If it’s just screen fodder, I’m not interested.” The fact that Wiggle Wood was accepted for formal research, and the results it produced, reflects the genuine quality of the content.

The Headline Result

The Tomorrow’s Child Assessment Tool measures attention and engagement at five stages across an episode: Introduction, Building Narrative, Mid Sequence, Climax, and Ending. Each stage is scored out of 12. The maximum total score is 60. A successful, high-performing children’s television show typically scores between 49 and 50. Wiggle Wood scored 12/12 at every stage - a total of 60/60. According to Dr Harding, this is the first time a perfect score has been awarded across her 20-plus years of research.

Observational evidence recorded throughout included sustained visual focus, strong emotional mirroring, consistent postural engagement, minimal distraction, and continued attention during the reflective closing sequence and credits. Engagement remained stable even during emotionally reflective, dialogue-heavy scenes - a significant finding.

Comprehension and Narrative Understanding

Children demonstrated unusually high comprehension on a single, first viewing, with no prior exposure to the characters or world. Ages 4–5: 80% comprehension. Ages 5–6: 75% comprehension. Ages 6–7: 80% comprehension. Children were able to recall narrative structure, describe emotional themes, explain character motivations, and articulate the concept of “wiggling it out” as emotional self-regulation.

From the documentary film produced alongside the formal report, Dr Harding observes: “Right from the very start, one thing became clear: this programme truly understands children. Not simply as viewers, not simply as consumers, but as young human beings whose brains, emotions and sense of self are still under construction. And that matters enormously.”

Humour and Music

Humour sequences consistently generated spontaneous, contagious laughter across all age groups and settings, with children mimicking moments and turning to each other to share the reaction. Dr Harding notes that this is developmentally significant: after genuine laughter, the nervous system settles and children become more receptive to learning. Children returned immediately to focused attention after each humorous moment.

Music emerged as a major strength. Children responded physically throughout - moving, wiggling, singing along. During slower, reflective sequences, researchers observed a shift to quiet stillness. The closing song, “Your Best Is Beautiful,” produced visible emotional responses in both children and the adult educators present.

The Wind-Down and Emotional Regulation

The mindful wind-down sequence at the close of the episode was identified as one of the programme’s most important differentiators. Researchers observed reduced post-viewing dysregulation compared to typical screen content, calm transitions following the episode, an absence of “screen fatigue” or overstimulation, and increased emotional stillness and reflective behaviour in children.

One educator described the value directly: “Just to have those little tricks up their sleeve to help them transform from one phase and feeling to another is a good strength for them to have. I love the fact that at the end of the programme it asks the children to close their eyes, to think of something, to listen. All emotions are real and valid, and being able to name them is important. But just as importantly, children need ways to deal with their emotions.”

Emotional Depth

The programme’s core emotional message - that it is safe to feel your feelings, and that you can move through them - was widely and spontaneously understood. On the central concept of “wiggling it out”, Dr Harding observed: “Children regulate through movement long before they regulate through reasoning. Their bodies help organise their brains. So when a child is invited to wiggle it out, they’re being given a practical, emotionally safe strategy their nervous system can genuinely use. That’s where the gold dust lies.”

One child experiencing emotional distress at the time of viewing described what the programme gave them as: “Taking the bad stuff out of my head.” This moment was not filmed, out of respect for the child. It was noted by Dr Harding as one of the most significant findings of the entire study.

SEND Representation

The research included meaningful representation of children with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities. In one placement, SEND representation reached 49% of the children present. The programme performed strongly across this group, with no meaningful difference in engagement or response compared to the wider cohort.

Character Connection

Children formed strong attachments to the characters on a single viewing. Bellona emerged as the most consistently preferred character across all age groups, followed closely by Chadwick. Younger children recalled character names readily; older children engaged more with personality, motivation, and narrative role - all indicators of genuine connection rather than surface recognition.

What Educators and Parents Said

Adult responses were overwhelmingly positive. Representative comments included: “Strong messaging for supporting mental health.” “Perfect content for discussion about not needing to be perfect.” “I could use Wiggle Wood to support PSHE plans.” “The music is outstanding.” Most adults described the programme as developmentally valuable, emotionally intelligent, distinct from mainstream children’s programming, and well suited to co-viewing.

Dr Harding’s Conclusion

From the formal research report, May 2026: “The evidence strongly suggests that Wiggle Wood has significant developmental and educational potential and represents a valuable contribution to children’s broadcasting.” And in her personal reflection: “Wiggle Wood occupies a unique and valuable place within contemporary children’s media. Children deserve media experiences that respect their intelligence, emotional depth, and developmental capacity.”

Context for Investors

Independent research of this quality is rare at any stage of production, let alone at pilot. The fact that a single episode, produced as a prototype, achieved a perfect engagement score, demonstrated comprehension rates of 75–80% across all tested age groups on first viewing, and created immediate emotional connection across children from diverse socioeconomic and SEN backgrounds, is a direct proof of concept. The creative and emotional architecture of Wiggle Wood is working exactly as intended. It is the foundation on which 156 episodes will be built.

Marketing Strategy

Wiggle Wood has been designed from the outset to scale rapidly and reach a global family audience. Marketing is therefore a core strategic growth driver, supported by a dedicated budget and long-term brand-building approach to accelerate awareness, drive audience acquisition, and establish Wiggle Wood as a leading international preschool franchise quickly.

Digital Advertising and Organic Growth

In the early stages, the marketing strategy will focus on driving external traffic - parents and caregivers - to YouTube through a combination of digital adverts, talent amplification, social media promotion, and selective collaborations with established creators and children’s brands. Key creative talent attached to Wiggle Wood brings significant existing reach across major platforms. Musical Director Jax Jones has 2.5 million followers across YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram, while actress Corinna Brown, who voices Chadwick, brings an engaged audience of more than 500,000 followers on Instagram.

Additional reach will be supported through cross-promotional opportunities with Little Amigo (over 700,000 subscribers), the flagship YouTube children’s channel operated by the Lasso Group. This collaboration will help introduce Wiggle Wood to an established preschool audience while reinforcing a consistent pipeline of platform-optimised content.

Paid Social Strategy

Wiggle Wood’s paid social strategy is managed by ViVV, a specialist digital marketing agency with deep experience scaling children’s and family brands across social platforms. ViVV have developed a three-year tiered investment plan covering TikTok, Facebook, Instagram, and YouTube, structured to flex with organic performance so that paid spend amplifies what is already working rather than compensating for what is not. Total three-year paid social investment is planned at between £787,500 and £1,390,500 depending on organic performance, with a fixed agency retainer rather than a percentage-of-spend model, ensuring ViVV’s incentives are always aligned with Wiggle Wood’s best interests.

Influencer Engagement

Alongside macro talent promotion, Wiggle Wood will also activate large-scale micro-influencer engagement through Hypefy, an AI-powered creator platform with access to more than 53 million content creators worldwide. This allows the marketing team to identify and collaborate with creators whose audiences closely align with Wiggle Wood’s core communities, including parents, caregivers, early years educators, and values-led families.

A key element of Wiggle Wood’s influencer strategy is the commissioning of creator-owned content for use as paid advertising. Rather than producing traditional brand adverts, Wiggle Wood pays influencers to create content in their own voice, talking authentically about the series to their own audiences. Wiggle Wood then acquires the rights to that content and uses it as paid advertising across social platforms, amplifying it far beyond the influencer’s own organic reach.
